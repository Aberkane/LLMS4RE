# Adopting Large Language Models for GDPR Compliance in User Stories

## Abstract
_Purpose:_ Assessing compliance with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) within requirements engineering (RE) remains challenging, particularly in agile contexts where requirements are expressed as user stories. The GDPR requires early consideration of data protection by design, yet evaluating user stories for compliance demands legal expertise that is often limited. Large Language Models (LLMs) may offer support, but empirical evidence on their usefulness and professional acceptance in RE is scarce. This study, therefore, investigates (1) the extent to which LLM-based GDPR compliance assessments of user stories align with expert evaluations and (2) the factors influencing professionals’ intention to adopt such tools. _Methods:_ A two-stage empirical design was employed. First, GDPR compliance assessments generated by four state-of-the-art LLMs were compared with expert panel evaluations across 33 user stories. Alignment was examined using the Mann–Whitney U test and effect size measures. Second, a survey of 51 RE and IT-law professionals assessed determinants of adoption using an extended Technology Acceptance Model incorporating perceived trust. _Results:_ Three of the four LLMs produced compliance assessments that did not differ significantly from expert evaluations, indicating substantial alignment in judgment. However, occasional deviations and hallucinations highlight the need for expert oversight. Adoption findings show that perceived usefulness and trust are the strongest predictors of behavioral intention to use LLM-based assessment tools. _Conclusion:_ The findings provide empirical evidence that LLMs can support GDPR compliance assessment in RE with expert-comparable performance while adoption depends primarily on perceived usefulness and trust. In privacy-sensitive contexts, the results suggest that LLMs are best positioned as complementary tools that support, rather than replace, professional judgment.
